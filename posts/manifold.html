Overview of Manifolds

Manifolds are spaces locally "like" Euclidean space $\RR^n$. We use the ambiguous word "like" because there are multiple aspects of Euclidean structure we may wish to mimic and thus multiple notions of manifold. Each type of structure is encoded in a notion of "morphism". When "morphism" means "continuous map", we obtain the notion of topological manifold. When "morphism" means "smooth map", we obtain the notion of smooth manifold. When "morphism" means "affine map", we obtain the notion of affine manifold. When "morphism" means "isometry", we obtain the notion of flat manifold. Then:

Definition: an $n$-manifold is a set of bijections $\phi_i:U_i\from R_i \subseteq \RR^n$ from open sets $R_i\subseteq \RR^n$ such that each $\phi_i^{-1} \circ \phi_j |_{\phi_j^{-1}(U_i)}$ is an "isomorphism".

Definition: Manifolds form a concrete category as follows. It is the union $X=\bigcup_i U_i$ that we consider each manifold's underlying set. If $M, M'$ are $m$- and $m'$- manifolds, then $f:M'\from M$ is a morphism when each ${\phi'}_i^{-1} \circ f \circ \phi_j |_{(f\circ \phi_j)^{-1}({U'}_i)}$ is a "morphism".

The topological notion is most general. In fact, we may equip $X$ with the unique topology that makes each $\phi_i$ a homeomorphism. Then $X$ is a topological space. It is easy to check that the morphisms between topological manifolds are the continuous maps. Because we aim to do calculus on manifolds, we would like unique limits and therefore prefer the case that $X$ is Hausdorff. Classification questions also interest us; however, even the classification of $0$-manifolds is difficult as stated: there is a $0$-manifold for each cardinality! So let us consider only manageably small manifolds: the ones with countable basis. In short, we will require henceforth that manifolds be Hausdorff and second-countable.

Examples of Manifolds

To classify manifolds, it's enough to classify connected manifolds. Then every general manifold will be a countable union of connected manifolds.

The only connected $0$-manifold is the point. There's only one topological structure, only one smooth structure, only one affine structure, and only one flat structure.

The only connected topological or smooth $1$-manifolds are the circle and the open interval. But! There are uncountably many affine $1$-manifolds: a single open interval and an uncountable family of circles (parameterized by how tangent vectors stretch as we go around). And there are uncountably many flat $1$-manifolds: each finite size of circle, each finite size of open interval, and a single open line, and a single open ray.

Riemann Manifolds

Since the smooth manifold $\RR^1$ (hence $\RR^n$) has multiple flat structures, it makes sense to generalize the notion of "flat manifold". Since the morphisms of flat manifolds are isometries, we'd like to consider a larger collection of metric spaces as manifolds. However, by insisting that the global metric $\delta:M^2\to [0,\infty]$ be smooth, we rule out desirable examples such as the circle. Indeed, the arclength metric on $S^1$ is not smooth when evaluated between antipodal points. Our eventual definition will be equivalent to the following: a Riemannian manifold is a smooth manifold together with a global metric $\delta:M^2\to [0, \infty]$ that induces the topological structure and is locally smooth. That is, for any $p\in M$, there must exist a neighborhood $p\in U\subseteq M$ such that $\delta|_{U^2}$ is smooth. The morphisms will be smooth isometries.

Riemann Manifolds

To move from topology to geometry, we need concepts such as length, angle, and volume. It turns out that we can recover the other concepts from lengths. Indeed, recall that every isometry of Euclidean space also preserves angles and has determinant $\pm 1$. We can explicitly solve for angles (between two vectors) in terms of lengths from the generalized Pythagorean Theorem: $$ \|v\|^2 + \|w\|^2 = \|v-w\|^2 + 2\|v\| \|w\|\cos(\theta) $$ Once equipped with length and angle, we can recognize which parallelepipeds are right-angled, define their volumes as the products of their side-lengths, and thus recover volume.

The previous paragraph dealt with vectors and hence local, "microscopic" geometry. However, we may also wonder about global geometric concepts such as the distance between two points. One can move from local to global distance as follows: define the length of a curve as the sum of the lengths of its microscopic segments; define the distance between two points as the infimum of lengths of curves between them. Curves that achieve that infimum are geodesics.

A final amazing bit of geometry: the notion of geodesic allows us to compare disparate points. For instance, suppose a quadrilateral of geodesics is a rhombus, that is, opposite sides have equal lengths. Then we might say that the opposite sides are parallel. This definition suffers as stated. For example, consider $S^2$ with the usual geometry, and choose two north-pointing vectors at points $a, b$ on the equator. They generate geodesics that go all the way to the North Pole $n$. Then $a, b, n$ form a triangle whose north-pointing sides are parallel'' from the equatorial segment $ab$'s perspective, but not at all from the rhombus definition. It turns out that the rhombus picture only makes sense for infinitesimal sides. This still allows us to compare infinitesimally distant tangent spaces. By integrating, we may parallel transport vectors along a curve. And by subtracting, we may covariantly differentiate a vector field with respect to another vector.

Is Every Space Flat?

$\RR$ and $S^1$ have flat structures, so $\RR^n \times (S^1)^m$ has flat structure. But the sphere $S^2$ has no flat structure. Moreover, though every metric structure on $\RR^1$ is flat, some metric structures on $\RR^2$ are curved.
