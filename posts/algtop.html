What is Algebraic Topology?

Invariants Help Us Distinguish Spaces

A classic technique for showing that two things are different is to demonstrate a property of one that doesn't hold for another. For instance, this trick allows us to show that a circle and a real line are not homemorphic: one is compact, and the other isn't! To show that five circles are not homeomorphic to ten circles, we can't invoke compactness, but we can count connected components. However, other distinctions require subtler tools to see. No elementary topological property distinguishes a one-holed torus from a two-holed torus, for example. Instead of associating a truth-value or number with each space, it turns out in this case to be more fruitful to associate a group. Algebraic topology is the study of such algebraically flavored topological invariants.

Homotopy Equivalence

We will investigate two invariants: the fundamental group and singular homology. Both are preserved under not only homeomorphism but also homotopy equivalence. It is easier for spaces to be homotopy equivalent than to be homeomorphic: the latter implies the former. So the invariant technique cannot always distinguish nonhomeomorphic spaces. The technique can't distinguish spaces that are too similar to each other, but when it does distinguish spaces, we know that they are very dissimilar. Moreover, the invariants we will study are not even sufficient to reconstruct spaces up to homotopy equivalence. This means two things: that we might still confuse very dissimilar spaces, and also that the converse problem of showing that these tools cannot show that two spaces are the same.

The Fundamental Group

The Fundamental Set

For a space $X$, consider the continuous maps $:X\from S^1$. Recall that two such maps $f,g$ are homotopic when there is some continuous interpolator $h:X\from [0,1]\times S^1$ such that $h_0=f$ and $h_1=g$. This is clearly an equivalence relation. The resulting equivalence classes we will call loops in $X$. The set $F(X)$ of loops is X's fundamental set.

For instance, $F(S^1)$ is infinite, while $F(\RR)$ is finite of size $1$. Since $F$ is clearly invariant under homeomorphism, this provides another proof that $S^1$ and $\RR$ are not homeomorphic.

Likewise, $F(S^2)$ is finite of size $1$, while $F(S^1\times S^1)$ is infinite. This shows that the sphere and torus are not homeomorphic.

However, $F(T^{\#1})$ and $F(T^{\#2})$ are both countably infinite. We need a finer invariant to distinguish them.

Before we do so, let's think about the fundamental set. First, it is a functor from Top to Set. It acts on morphisms in the natural way: if $s:X\from Y$ is continuous, then $F(s)$ sends the loop $f:Y\from S^1$ to the loop $s\circ f:X\from S^1$.

Thus, injections map to injections, so $S^1$ is not a deformation retract of the disk $D^2$.

The Fundamental Group

To obtain a more powerful invariant, we needn't look far. The idea is to consider relationships between loops, thus imposing further structure on the Fundamental Set. In fact, we will modify our definitions slightly to allow composition of loops, yielding the Fundamental Group. We can consider loops as paths when we fix a basepoint $\star\in S^1$. Then, if $f,g:X\from S^1$ are loops with $f(\star)=g(\star)$, we can compose the loops into $(fg):X\from S^1$ using the homotopy class of maps $:S^1\wedge S^1\from S^1$.
