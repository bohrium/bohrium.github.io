Sneaky Motivation for Quantum Computation
The discreteness of combinatorial situations can lead to a frustrating lack of flexibility: to progress, one feels forced to break symmetries, do casework, and reason about obstinate constraint satisfaction problems. But sometimes, by considering weighted averages of elements of discrete sets, one can do better. Thus, the standard proof of Sperner-type Lemmas pass through linear algebra intermediates. To analyze finite groups, one may consider their linear representations. The continuous, linear-algebra perspective gives powerful tools such as Kirchoff's Matrix Tree Theorem.

We thus wonder whether the digital logic of classical circuits can be made more powerful by linearization. Instead of circuits $f: \{0,1\}^n\to\{0,1\}^m$, let us consider linear operators $A: \CC^{(2^n)}\to \CC^{(2^m)}$. It is encouraging that the state spaces of quantum mechanics are from some viewpoints free spaces generated by classical states. However, in order to maintain the fragile superposition of states in $\CC^{2^\cdots}$, the machine must be insulated from thermal randomness. But it is precisely thermal effects that allow classical gates such as AND to destroy information at macroscopic scales. In the absence of this source of irreversibility, we must analyze our physical systems as reversible. Therefore, our quantum machines will have $n=m$, and $A$ will be a linear automorphism of $\CC^{2^n}$.
