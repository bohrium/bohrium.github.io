What are Representations?

Yeah, What are They?

A representation is a group homomorphism $\pi:G\to \Aut(V)$ from a group to the linear automorphisms of a vector space. We say that $\pi$ represents $G$ or is a representation of $G$ on $V$. We will often abuse terminology by speaking of "the representation $V$", even though $V$ technically lacks the information of $\pi$; in doing so, we sin as heinously yet conveniently as when we refer to a group by its underlying set. We thus speak of a representation's dimension (i.e. the dimension of its underlying vector space $V$) and subspaces and so forth. This document will treat complex representations only, i.e. those such that $V$ is a vector space over $\CC$.

For example, if $G$ acts on a set $X$, then this action induces a representation of $G$ on the free vector space $\CC^X$. Concretely, this means that each element $g\in G$ maps to a permutation matrix. Thus, though every vector space is a set (via the forgetful functor) and thus representation theory a special case of group action theory, we can also say that, because every set generates a vectorspace (via the free functor), that group action theory is a special case of representation theory. As a special case, if $X=G$ and $G$ acts on $X$ by left multiplication, then we obtain a representation of $G$ on $\CC^G$ --- this is the regular representation.

Finite Cyclic Groups

Not all representations have permutation-matrix form: let $G = \ZZ/n\ZZ$ act on the 1-dimensional complex vectorspace $V=\CC^1$ by $\pi_\eta(g)(v) = \eta^g v$, where $\eta$ is a fixed $n$th root of unity. The corresponding matrices have shape 1-by-1 and entry $\eta^g$. It's easy to show that the representations of $\ZZ/n\ZZ$ on 1-dimensional complex vector spaces are in correspondence with the $n$ different $n$th roots of unity.

From the $\pi_\eta$ s, we can form higher-dimensional representations via direct sum. To perform direct sum on representations of a fixed group $G$, just perform it on their underlying vector spaces and let $G$ act componentwise. In fact, all finite-dimensional representations of $\ZZ/n\ZZ$ arise this way! Of course, to understand what the previous sentence means, we must define what it means for two representations to be the same. Well, a homomorphism of representations is just a homomorphism of vector spaces that commutes with the group action. And an isomorphism is a bijective homomorphism --- it's easy to show that its inverse will also be a homomorphism. Then the direct sum we defined earlier is just a coproduct in the resulting category. In short, we claim:

Theorem M: Each finite-dimensional representation $\pi$ of $G=\ZZ/n\ZZ$ is isomorphic to some $\bigoplus_i \pi_{\eta_i}$.

Proof: If $g\in G$, then $\pi(g)^n=I$ is the identity. However, a consideration of Jordan Normal Form shows that this is possible only if $\pi(g)$ is diagonalizable. Moreover, since $G$ is abelian, the $\pi(g)$s all commute. Now, if a set of diagonalizable operators all commute, then there exists a basis that simultaneously diagonalizes all of them. This basis gives the decomposition we seek. (end of Proof)

In fact, the proof shows that whenever $G$ is a finite abelian group, then its finite-dimensional representations into 1-dimensional representations.
