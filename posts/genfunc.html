Generating Functions for Counting

If $a\in \NN^\NN$ is a sequence of non-negative integers, then its associated generating series is $F_a(z) = \sum \in \sum_{n\in \NN}a_n z^n \in \CC[[z]]$, an element of the formal power series ring. For many interesting $a$, $F_a$ has positive radius of convergence and hence may be viewed as a function.

Example: Fun with Geometric Series

For instance, $a_n=1$ imples that $F_a=1/(1-z)$. Then $a_n=n+1$ implies that $F_a=1/(1-z)^2$.

Example: Solving Linear Recurrences

For instance, let $a$ be defined by a linear recurrence: $a_n = s_n$ for $0\leq n \le N$ and $a_{n+N} = \sum_{0\leq i\le N} c_i a_{n+i}$ for $n\in \NN$. Then $F_a$ obeys the relation: $F_a(z) = \sum_{0\leq n\le N} a_n z^n + \sum_{0\leq i\le N} c_i z^{N-i} = S(z) + C(z) F_a(z)$. So the following equality of functions holds when both sides are defined: $$F_a(z) = \frac{S(z)}{1-C(z)}$$ Via partial fraction decomposition, we write $F_a(z) = \sum_{0\leq i\le N} p_i(z)/(z-\lambda_i)$ with each $p_i\in \CC[z]$ and $\lambda_i\in \CC$. We recognize this as a sum of geometric series. We conclude that $F_a$'s coefficients $a_n$ will eventually be a sum of exponentials! We could also have seen this by diagonalizing the relevant linear transform. But generating functions emphasize the sequential nature of the problem; they can thus give more specific insights.
